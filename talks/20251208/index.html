• Thank you for the kind introduction and for inviting me to speak at the UCSD RNA Center.
  • It is nice to see many friends and colleagues in San Diego.
  • I have already learned lot of fun science and hoping to do more of that today.

  • Inside our cells, RNA, and specifically mRNA go through several complex metabolic steps.
  • After transcription, mRNAs are capped, spliced, and polyadenylated in the nucleus, and then exported to the cytoplasm.
  • In the cytoplasm, mRNAs can be transported to specific organelles, and are translated into proteins, can be stored as condensates, and are eventually degraded.
  • Even though the mRNA lifecycle drawn this way looks linear and ordered, we know that it is more complex in cells.

  • Each mRNA processing or metabolic step does not occur in isolation, but is often in kinetic competition with other steps.
  • In particular, if any of these individual steps is inefficient, cells have numerous quality control mechanisms which degrade the RNA, prevent them from being translated, or degrade the protein that is produced off the RNA.

  • My lab is interested in understanding the kinetic interplay between these different RNA metabolic and quality control events within the cell, with a specific focus on mRNA translation.
  • We have taken three distinct approaches to this question that I am going to cover in my talk today.

  • 18 of the 20 amino acids can be encoded in the mRNA by multiple codons.
  • for example, leucine can be encoded by 6 different synonymous codons.

  • By using ribosome profiling, a method for measuring ribosome occupancy across all mRNAs,
  • We found that starvation for an amino acid results in very different levels of ribosome stalling at the corresponding codons.
  • For example, you see here that leucine starvation causes ribosome pausing only at 3 of the 6 synonymous codons for leucine.
  • We also found if we use different synonymous codons for leucine in fluorescent preorter genes, we get very different levels of protein expression during starvation, even though we saw no different during normal growth.
  • These observations led us to think about the questions how exactly ribosome stalling reduces protein expression.

  • We realized that if we vary the initiation rate of ribosomes on mRNAs with stalls in our simulations, the effect of the stall on protein expression should remain constant in this simple model of abortive termination.

  • However, our experiments gave a very different result.
  • When we changed the rate of ribosome loading by mutating the Shine-Dalgarno or the start codon of the mRNA in E. coli, we saw that the stall’s effect on protein expression was diminished at lower initiation rates.
  • So, we had to come up with a new model that could explain this data.

  • So we set about measuring the effect of ribosome collisions on protein expression in the budding yeast, S. cerevisiae, by inserting a poly lysine motif that is known to cause ribosome stalling into a fluorescent reporter.
  • We varied the initiation rate in this case by changing the Kozak sequence preceding the start codon.
  • We saw that as we make the Kozak sequence on a control mRNA stronger, we get more protein expression, as you see in grey here.
  • But when we do the same for the stall-containing mRNA, shown in yellow here, we see that protein expression actually goes down at higher initiation rates.

  • We realized that our data could be readily explained if abortive termination does not occur on stalled ribosomes, but only when a second ribosome collides with the leading stalled ribosome.
  • In this model, the stall’s effect will be be diminished at lower initiation rates because the rate of collision goes down quadratically with the initiation rate.
  • Even more importantly, the collision model is highly selective for the stall-containing mRNAs, independent of the rate of abortive termination. So, it immediately solves the problem of selectivity as well.

  • We found that the collision-stimulated model fits the experimental data on initiation rate much better.
  • We did a number of additional experiments to test this model, which I am not showing here. For example, we changed the type of stall, the number of stalls, and the distance between stalls.
  • In each case, we saw that the collision-stimulated abortive termination model fit the measurements better than simple abortive termination model.
  • We did all this work in E. coli, but the collision model seemed so general that we wanted to test it in a eukaryotic system.

  • We noticed the same at the level of mRNA stability too where these stall-containing mRNAs shown in yellow actually become less stable at higher initiation rates.
  • One technical point which I will come back to again in my talk: To accurately measure mRNA levels in a pooled fashion, we developed a barcoding strategy where we inserted random barcodes into the 3’UTRs and linked them to kozak and stall mutations by sequencing the plasmid.
  • After we intergrate these barcoded plasmids as a pool into the yeast genome, we can calculate the relative mRNA levels by simply counting the barcode in the cDNA and genomic DNA.

  • To compare these experiment results to a computational model of ribosome collisions, we had to consider the kinetics of eukaryotic translation and stabiility carefully.
  • I won’t go into the technical details much here, but we could do this modeling by adapting a a very nice framework called rule-based or agent-based modeling from the cell signaling field.
  • In this method, if you list the molecules and their reactions in a computer-readable format, then you can use a general purpose simulator to track the time evolution of the system and calculate various observables like protein expression and mRNA stability.

  • For example, we used this formalism to simulate different types of abortive termination models where ribosome collisions cause either the leading ribosome or the trailing ribosome to fall off or trigger mRNA cleavage.
  • It turns out that only if the leading ribosome is removed by collision, we can explain the lower protein expression and mRNA stability that we see at higher initiation rates in our experiments.
  • So, you can see how combining reporter assays with computational modeling can help us make very specific predictions about the molecular events that occur at stalled ribosomes.

  • When we published our first study on ribosome collisions in 2016, no one in the translation field cared much because we did not have the the protein factors that sense ribosome collisions.
  • But since then, many groups have been finding several factors that are associated with collided ribosomes in both bacteria and eukaryotes.
  • They fall into 2 broad classes:
    • Factors like SmrB or MutS2 in bacteria and Hel2 or ZNF598 in eukaryotes bind collided ribosomes and regulate the expression in cis on only the stall-containing mRNA.
    • Then factors like GCN1 or ZAK bind collided ribosomes and trigger a global response such as the integrated stress response or apoptosis.
    • Manu Hegde’s group has even found that the splitting factor called ASCC3 that acts on the leading ribosome in a collision.

  • We had done all our experiments in bacteria and yeast where we knew very well what sequence motifs trigger ribosome stalling and quality control.
  • But we did not know much about the sequence motifs that trigger ribosome stalling in human cells.
  • So we developed a massively parallel approach to identify these motifs that I want to talk about next.

  • Our assay is very similar to the one I had previously shown in yeast, but now at a much larger scale.
  • We cloned 8 tandem repeats of all 4096 codon pairs in the middle of a reporter mRNA along with 15 random barcodes without stop codons for each insert.
  • We integrated these reporters into the genome of HEK293T cells at a defined locus using CRISPR/Cas9 mediated HDR.
  • By measuring the ratio of barcode counts for each insert between the cDNA and the genomic DNA, we can accurately infer the effect of each codon pair on steady-state mRNA level.

  • As a positive control, we found that stop codons in our inserts decrease mRNA level, likely because premature termination codons trigger nonsense-mediated mRNA decay.
  • On average, the positively charged amino acids, lysine and arginine, decrease mRNA levels more than any of the other amino acids in our reporter assay.

  • Surprisingly, we also identified several specific dipeptide repeats that decrease mRNA stability.
  • When we looked at individual amino acid pairs, apart from a few exceptions, it was mainly combinations of positively charged amino acids like lysine or arginine with bulky or hydrophobic amino acids such as tyrosine and valine that decrease mRNA levels.

  • We then noticed that for dipeptides containing positively charged amino acids, their effect on mRNA stability is correlated with the propensity of the second amino acid to form β-strand secondary structure.
  • This is quantitified by these Chou-Fasman parameters inferred from known protein structures.
  • We did not see the same correlation with α-helix propensities for these amino acids, suggesting that β-strand propensity is a key determinant of ribosome stalling and mRNA stability in human cells.

  • To test the causal role of β-strand propensity on translation, we combined two different dipeptides encoded by the four amino acids S, V, K, and F in several different orders and repeated them 4 times.
  • These dipeptides are computationally predicted to form either β-strand or α-helix with high probability, that we also confirmed in vitro assays.
  • We then inserted the coding sequences of the α-helix and β-strand forming motifs upstream of a luciferase reporter, and performed in vitro translation.
  • We saw that the reporters with β-strand forming inserts produced luciferase slower than the ones that had α-helix forming inserts.
  • This is consistent with β-strand forming peptides slowing down ribosomes relative to α-helix forming peptides.

  • This pattern very nicely recapitulates the known residue requirements based on cryo-EM structure of a known stalling peptide from the SDD1 gene in yeast.
  • Specifically, we think that the positive charge perturbs the peptidyl-transferase center, and the bulky amino acid interacts with the constriction point in the exit tunnel.
  • These experiments hopefully give you a sense of how we can use massively parallel reporter assays to understand the mRNA sequence determinants of quality control.

  • In the third and final part of my talk, I want to show how we can combine these reporter assays with CRISPR screens to identify trans regulators of virtually any step of RNA metabolism in human cells.
  • There are over 2,000 RNA-binding proteins in the human genome, and it often a challenge to figure out which protein regulates which RNA metabolic step.

  • CRISPR screens are a popular way to identify genes that regulate RNA metabolism.
  • Typically, an RNA metabolic step such as splicing is coupled to a fluorescent reporter,
  • Cells expressing the reporter are perturbed by a genome-wide CRISPR library, and are sorted and sequenced based on the fluorescence of the reporter.
  • The problem with this approach is that we are looking at protein expression, which might not necessarily change when an RNA metabolic step is perturbed, or might change for reasons that have nothing to do with RNA metabolism. So, basically you tend to have high false positives and negatives.

  • A recent alternative is to do Perturb-seq, where you do single cell RNA sequencing after CRISPR perturbations.
  • But single cell screens are limited to highly expressed endogenous mRNAs, they typically sequence only 3’ ends of RNAs and are not flexible, and are also really expensive to use for more than a handful of genetic perturbations.

  • To get around this problem, We have developed an approach called RNA-linked CRISPR, or ReLiC for short, that enables us to directly measure RNA phenotypes while targeting every known RNA-associated protein in human cells.
  • There are two key ideas in ReLiC.
  • One is that instead of cloning a fluorescent tag, you put a short 20nt random barcode in your reporter.
  • Two, instead of delivering the CRISPR guide library in trans, you put it in cis along with the barcoded reporter.
  • What this lets you do is to link each random barcode to a unique guide RNA.
  • You then integrate this library as a pool into HEK293T cells, induce Cas9 to deplete the targeted proteins, and then sequence the barcodes in the reporter to measure RNA phenotypes.
  • ReLiC is super flexible, and lets us look at pretty much any RNA metabolic process like translation, mRNA decay, or splicing that has a sequencing-based readout.
  • I want to show you three examples of how we have used it so far.

  • First, we use ReLiC to identify isoform-specific splicing regulators of a β-globin reporter with three exons and two introns.
  • We integrated the guide RNA-reporter library into HEK293T cells, and extracted the RNA after Cas9 induction.
  • In the final PCR step before sequencing, simply by changing where the PCR primer binds, we can do an intron retention screen for either of the two introns or an exon skipping screen by using a primer that bridges exons 1 and 3.
  • We then look for guides that increase the ratio of the intron-retained or the exon-skipped isoform to the total mRNA, which is the splicing phenotype we are measuring.

  • This is a busy volcano plot showing the results, so let me walk you through it.
  • The y-axis is the false discovery rate, the x-axis is the isoform ratio, the three rows correspond to the different isoforms, and the four columns correspond to different days after Cas9 dinctuion.
  • We get a lot of information from these measurements, but I want to highlight 3 interesting patterns.
  • First, you see that intron 2 splicing is much more sensitive than intron 1 splicing or exon 2 skipping to depletion of RNA-associate proteins.
  • Second, when you look at what are the proteins that cause intron retention, it is pretty much all known splicing-associated proteins.
  • Third, you see that exon skipping is promoted by depletion of a very specific set of splicing factors, most of which are associated with the U2 snRNP.

  • Next, we used ReLiC to study regulators of nonsense-mediated mRNA decay, in human cells.
  • For this, we slightly modified our β-globin reporter to include a premature termination codon in the second exon, that is known to trigger NMD.
  • We also put a second barcode on a control reporter.
  • We then looked guides that increase the barcodes of the PTC-containing mRNA relative to a second barcode on a control reporter.

  • We find about 100 hits for the PTC reporter, but almost none for the control reporter.
  • Among the hits for the PTC, we recover the known core NMD factors like the UPF and SMG proteins.
  • But we also get a whole set of ribosomal proteins, ribosome biogenesis factors, translation factors, and tRNA synthetases that are rarely found in fluorescent reporter screens for NMD.
  • This makes sense because if you knock down translation components, you are not going to get increased protein expression no matter whether or not the mRNA is an NMD target.

  • Surprisingly, when we look at the effect of individual translation initiation factors on our NMD reporter, we see that knocking down eIF2 and eIF2B subunits increases the mRNA level, but depletion of all eIF4F and most eIF3 subunit has almost no effect on NMD.

  • Since we know that many stresses cause eIF2 phosphorylation, we wanted to ask which of the hits in our screen regulate NMD through eIF2.

  • For this, we repeated ReLiC with our NMD reporter, but now in the presence of the drug ISRIB that prevents eIF2-mediated translational repression or added lentiviruses that target GCN2, an eIF2 kinase.
  • On the left, we see that treatment with ISRIB modifies the effect of both tRNA synthetases, mitochondrial hits, and several other elongation factors on the NMD reporter, showing that these hits work through eIF2 phosphorylation.
  • On the right, we see that knockout of GCN2 mainly modifies the effect of tRNA synthetases on the NMD reporter, showing that their depletion affects NMD through GCN2 signaling.
  • Hopefully, this gives you a sense of ReLiC is useful not just to identify regulators, but also dissect the pathways through which the regulators act.

  • The third example I want to show you today is how we can use ReLiC to study mRNA translation.
  • For this, we use polysome fractionation, a classic ultracentrifugation technique to separate mRNAs based on how many ribosomes are loaded.
  • We then sequence the barcodes from monosome and polysome fractions to identify knockouts that alter their ratio, which is a measure of ribosome occupancy on our reporter.

  • These are the results.
  • On the x-axis, you’re seeing the magnitude of the change in polysome-to-monosome ratio relative to the median knockdown.
  • On the y-axis, you’re seeing the significance of that change.
  • Out of the 2,000 RNA-associated proteins we knocked down, about 300 of them significantly reduced ribosome occupancy on our reporter.
  • A smaller fraction — 37 — increased it.

  • As expected, we see that knockdown of pretty much every ribosomal protein and every known ribosome biogenesis factor reduces the polysome to monosome ratio.

  • We also see that knockdown of most translation initiation factors reduce the polysome to monosome ratio.
  • More surprisingly, we see very different effects when we knockout essential protein complexes outside the core translation machinery.
  • When we knockdown proteasomal or TRiC chaperonin subunits, we see that polysome to monosome ratio decreases, but we don’t see any effect on translation when we perturb Pol II or essential splicing complexes.

- We can look at this relationship between essentiality and translation more broadly since we measure both barcode depletion after Cas9 induction and polysome distribution. - It is commonly assumed that if a gene is essential, its knowndown will decrease translation. - It is generally true that bigger the effect on growth, bigger the effect on translation. - But this coupling depends on which pathway you are perturbing. - For example, depletin gribosome components has a huge effect on translation, but depleting RNAP has no effect, and many other essential proteins are in between.

  • Polysome ReLiC also shows opposite effects of knocking down factors that act at initiation or elongation steps of translation.
  • We see that knockdown of EIF5A or EEF2 increases polysome to monosome ratio, but knocking down initiation factors like EIF2 or EIF3 decreases this ratio.

- We can also use ReLiC to study mRNA decay with translation inhibiting drugs, which again you cannot do with a fluorescent protein reporter. - As an example, we took a simple EYFP reporter, and performed a ReLiC screen to measure its RNA levels when we inhibited translation using either harringtonine that inhibits initiating ribosomes or cycloheximide which inhibits elongating ribosomes.

- Unlike our previous ReLiC screens, where we found dozens of hits and pathways, here we got a single unexpected hit for harringtonine, but not for cycloheximide. - We found that the knocking out the protein GCN1, decreases RNA levels specifically during harringtonine treatment.

- We know that GCN1 binds colliding ribosomes and can stimulate CCR4-NOT dependent decay. - But, harringtonine itself arrests initiating ribosomes but not elongating ribosomes. So, there should be no colliding ribosomes under harringtonine treatment. - So why does GCN1 show up as a hit here?

- We see that GCN1 knockout triggers upgregulation of immediate early genes during harringtonine treatment and also p38 phoshorylation, all in accordance with current models.

- One of the hypothesis we are trying to test is whether GCN1 is recruited to mRNAs with multiple start codons. - In this case, during harringtonine, elongating ribosomes can get stuck between the two initiating ribosomes and trigger collisions. - In fact, when you look at the ribosome density on the 5'UTR of immediate early genes like MYC and JUN, you see that full of initiating ribosomes at ATGs or near cognates.
